Step 1

What’s the Point?

1 What’s the Point?

State your claim clearly and upfront.

  • Lead with the conclusion, not the backstory
  • One sentence. Crisp. Direct.
  • Your audience should immediately know what you’re arguing

Bad

“So I was looking at the neonatal mortality data, and there are some interesting patterns in Malawi, and if we compare the staffing ratios with other countries…”

Good

“We need to recruit 200 additional nurse-midwives in Malawi’s high-burden facilities by year-end.”

The “Headline Test”

Ask yourself: if your point were a newspaper headline, would it make sense on its own?

Fails the test Passes the test
“There are some staffing challenges” “Malawi has a 65% vacancy rate for nurse-midwife technicians”
“We should think about the product rollout” “We need to deploy the PPH bundle across all 120 target facilities this quarter”
“Neonatal outcomes could be better” “80% of neonatal deaths are caused by three preventable conditions”

Step 2

Why It’s True

2 Why It’s True

Give the reasoning behind your point.

  • This is your logical backbone — the “because”
  • Connect cause to effect
  • Use data, logic, or first principles
  • Keep it tight: 1–3 supporting reasons max

Example

Point: “We need to recruit 200 additional nurse-midwives in Malawi’s high-burden facilities.”

Why it’s true: “Malawi has only 22 obstetricians and 1 neonatologist for 679,000 annual births. The vacancy rate for nurse-midwife technicians is 65%. You simply cannot deliver quality emergency obstetric care with these numbers.”

Strong vs. Weak Reasoning

Weak

  • “I feel like we need more staff”
  • “Other organizations are doing it”
  • “It’s the standard recommendation”
  • Relies on opinion or convention

Strong

  • “The vacancy rate is 65%”
  • “A 10% coverage increase averts 22% of maternal deaths”
  • “WHO and Lancet data both confirm this”
  • Relies on evidence and logic

Step 3

The Evidence — Zoom In & Zoom Out

3a Zoom In (Deduction)

Go deeper into a specific case.

  • Pick a concrete example that proves your point
  • Tell a short story: “When this happened before…”
  • Make the abstract tangible
  • Deductive reasoning: general rule → specific case

Example

“In one district hospital in Malawi, there were zero trained neonatal nurses covering a unit with 30 admissions per day. When a partner program placed just 4 mentored nurse-midwives there last year, the facility’s neonatal mortality dropped by 35% in six months. That’s what even a modest staffing increase looks like in practice.”

3b Zoom Out (Induction)

Pan out to see the bigger picture.

  • Show that this isn’t a one-off — it’s a pattern
  • Reference trends, comparisons, or analogies
  • “We’ve seen this across multiple cases…”
  • Inductive reasoning: multiple observations → general conclusion

Example

“This isn’t just one hospital. The Lancet’s modelling shows that a modest 10% increase in midwifery coverage every five years could avert 22% of maternal deaths, 23% of newborn deaths, and 14% of stillbirths globally — saving 1.35 million lives by 2035. With quality midwifery care, mothers are 24% less likely to experience preterm birth.”

Why You Need Both

Zoom In only

“One hospital saw a 35% drop…”

Audience thinks: “Is that a fluke?”

Zoom Out only

“The Lancet model projects…”

Audience thinks: “But does that apply to our context?”

Zoom In makes it real. Zoom Out makes it credible.

Use both to build an airtight case.

Step 4

Who Cares? (The Impact)

4 Who Cares? The Impact

Answer the question your audience is always silently asking:

“So what?”

  • Connect your point to what matters to them
  • Quantify the upside — or the cost of inaction
  • Make it feel urgent and relevant
  • This is what turns a good argument into a call to action

Making Them Care

Example

“If we fill these 200 nurse-midwife positions, our models estimate we can prevent over 3,000 maternal and newborn deaths per year in Malawi alone. If we don’t act, those positions stay vacant and those deaths remain preventable but unprevented. Every month of delay costs lives we have the tools to save.”

Pro tip: Frame impact in terms your audience cares about.

A government minister cares about national health indicators. A donor cares about lives saved per dollar. A facility director cares about staff workload and patient outcomes. Tailor the “who cares” to who’s in the room.

Putting It All Together

Full Example

1 The Point: “We need to recruit 200 additional nurse-midwives in Malawi’s high-burden facilities by year-end.”

2 Why it’s true: “Malawi has 1 neonatologist and a 65% nurse-midwife vacancy rate for 679,000 annual births. Quality care is impossible at these levels.”

3a Zoom in: “One district hospital placed 4 mentored nurse-midwives and saw neonatal mortality drop 35% in six months.”

3b Zoom out: “The Lancet shows a 10% increase in midwifery coverage averts 22% of maternal deaths and 23% of newborn deaths — 1.35M lives saved by 2035.”

4 Who cares: “Filling these positions prevents over 3,000 deaths per year in Malawi. Every month of delay costs lives we have the tools to save.”

Common Mistakes

  • Burying the point — Save the big reveal for movies, not meetings
  • Skipping the “why” — Assertions without reasoning don’t persuade
  • Only one type of evidence — One anecdote or one stat isn’t enough
  • Forgetting the “so what” — If you don’t spell out the impact, your audience won’t either
  • Going in the wrong order — Lead with the point, not the evidence

Adapt the Framework

You don’t always need all 4 steps at full depth.

Context What to emphasize
Quick meeting comment Steps 1 + 2 (point + why)
Formal presentation All 4 steps, in order
Email or Slack message Steps 1 + 4 (point + impact)
Persuading a skeptic Steps 2 + 3a + 3b (heavy on evidence)
Executive summary Steps 1 + 4 (lead with conclusion + stakes)

Key Takeaways

  1. Lead with the point — don’t make people guess
  2. Back it up with reasoning — the “because”
  3. Zoom In and Zoom Out — make it real and credible
  4. Land the impact — answer “who cares?”

A good point isn’t just correct — it’s complete.

Point → Reasoning → Evidence → Impact
